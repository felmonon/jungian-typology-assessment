Singer-Loomis vs MBTI: What's the Difference?

Why measuring all 8 cognitive functions independently provides more accurate personality assessment

January 15, 2026 • 12 min read • by TypeJung Team
Methodology Research Comparison

If you've taken a Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) assessment, you might have noticed something frustrating: you get different results each time. One day you're an INFJ, the next you're an INFP. The problem isn't you—it's the methodology.

The Problem with Forced-Choice Dichotomies

The MBTI and similar 4-letter type indicators work by forcing you to choose between binary opposites: Are you Thinking OR Feeling? Intuitive OR Sensing? Judging OR Perceiving?

This approach assumes that human psychology operates in mutually exclusive categories. But Carl Jung himself never intended his theory to work this way. In his 1921 work "Psychological Types," Jung described 8 cognitive functions that exist on a spectrum—present in everyone to varying degrees.

The key insight: You don't "have" Intuition OR Sensing. You have both. The question is: which do you prefer, and how strongly?

Enter the Singer-Loomis Type Deployment Inventory

In the 1980s, psychologists June Singer and Mary Loomis developed the Singer-Loomis Type Deployment Inventory (SLTDI), which addressed the fundamental flaws of forced-choice personality assessment.

Instead of asking "Are you A or B?", the Singer-Loomis methodology asks: "How much do you use A? How much do you use B?" It measures each of the 8 cognitive functions independently on a continuous scale.

Real-World Example

Consider two people who both test as "INFP" on the MBTI:

  • Person A: Strong Fi (85%), moderate Ne (70%), weak Si (40%), very weak Te (25%)
  • Person B: Strong Fi (80%), strong Ni (75%), weak Ne (45%), moderate Si (55%)

The MBTI gives them the same 4-letter code. But their cognitive profiles are quite different:

  • Person A has a clear Ne-Si axis (extraverted intuition vs introverted sensing)
  • Person B has unusually strong Ni for an INFP, suggesting possible mistyping or unique development

This is why you get different results on different MBTI tests. The forced-choice format can't capture your true cognitive complexity.

What the Research Shows

The Singer-Loomis methodology has been used in:

  • Clinical psychology settings since the 1980s
  • Academic research on personality development
  • Jungian analysis and depth psychology

Its independent measurement approach aligns with modern psychometric standards and provides:

  • Higher test-retest reliability
  • More nuanced personality profiles
  • Meaningful data on function development
  • Individualized growth recommendations
